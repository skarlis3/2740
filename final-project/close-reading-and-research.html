Close Reading, Analysis & Scholarly Research

Two ways to bring lens, research, and close reading together

Three Things to Bring Together

You have three things to bring together in your final project: a critical lens, scholarly research, and close reading. There are two ways you can structure how they fit together — pick whichever works better for your project.

Option 1: Lens as a Dedicated Page

Make a page on your site that introduces the critical lens you're using (feminism, new historicism, etc.). On this page:

  • Explain what the lens is

    Define the framework and the kinds of questions it asks.

  • Cite scholarly sources that back up your explanation

    Foundational thinkers, key concepts, or current scholarship in that tradition. Alternatively, connect literary criticism articles on the related framework to explain the lens. (For example, if you found an article on The Hunger Games and feminism in YA literature, you could explain the concepts from the article that broadly apply to young adult literature and feminism, even though you aren't specifically looking at The Hunger Games).

  • Connect this to your text(s)

    Broadly connect the lens to the text(s) you are analyzing for this project. For example, how does it fit these texts? Or what does it help a reader see differently?

Your close reading then lives on its own page (or pages) and uses the lens. You can refer back to ideas from the lens page without explaining them again.

Option 2: Mix Research Into Your Close Reading

Bring scholarly sources right into your close reading as you go. Instead of explaining the lens upfront, you introduce ideas and cite scholars at the moments they become relevant to what you're analyzing.

For Example

If you're doing a feminist reading and you reach a passage about a female character's silence, that's where you might bring in a scholar who's written about silence as a gendered move. The lens shows up through the analysis instead of being announced first.
