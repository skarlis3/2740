MLA Citations

Works Cited, in-text citations, and crediting sources on your site

What This Covers

This page covers how to handle citations in your final project: what goes in your Works Cited, when you need formal in-text citations, how to credit multimedia on your site, and a ready-to-use Works Cited list of all the course readings you can copy from directly.

Works Cited

Your Works Cited page lists every source you used in your project — readings you analyzed, scholarly sources, videos you embedded, images you included, everything. This is true for multimodal projects just like it is for essays. Even if a video is embedded right there on your page, it still needs an entry in your Works Cited.

Formatting

  • Title

    Center the words “Works Cited” at the top of the page. Don’t bold, underline, or enlarge it.

  • Alphabetical order

    Entries are sorted alphabetically by the first word (usually the author’s last name). Ignore “A,” “An,” and “The” when sorting titles.

  • Hanging indent

    The first line of each entry is flush left. Any additional lines are indented. (On Google Sites, you may need to approximate this with spacing — it doesn’t have to be perfect.)

  • Include working links

    For any online source, include a working URL at the end of the entry. Since this is a website project, your readers should be able to click through to verify your sources.

Citing Sources on Your Site

Since your final project is a website — not a traditional essay — how you reference sources depends on how you’re using them. The key principle is findability: it should always be clear what you’re discussing, and a reader should be able to connect anything on your site back to an entry on your Works Cited page.

When to Use Formal In-Text Citations

Use formal MLA in-text citations when you are quoting, paraphrasing, or referencing specific claims from a source in your own analytical writing — the same situations where you’d cite in an essay.

Quoting:

Douglass writes, “You have seen how a man was made a slave; you shall see how a slave was made a man” (39).

Paraphrasing:

Douglass describes his fight with Covey as the turning point that restored his sense of humanity (39).

This applies to your close reading section and any other writing where you’re making analytical claims supported by evidence from sources.

When Informal Credit Is Enough

When you’re presenting multimedia on your site — an embedded video, an image, a link to an external resource — you don’t need a formal parenthetical citation. But you do need to make it clear what it is and who made it so that a reader can find it on your Works Cited page.

Think of it this way: if the link broke tomorrow, could I still figure out what you were showing me and find it myself? If yes, your credit is sufficient.

For a YouTube video you’ve embedded: mention the title and/or the creator when you introduce or discuss it.

In the documentary Mr. SOUL!, directed by Melissa Haizlip, the show is described as a platform for political expression and cultural celebration.

For an image: include a caption or nearby text that identifies the artist/photographer, the title (if it has one), and where it came from.

Jacob Lawrence, The Migration Series, Panel 1 (1940–41). Museum of Modern Art.

For a link to an external source: use the title or author’s name in your text so it’s clear what you’re linking to.

The point isn’t to be formal — it’s to be clear. A reader should always know what they’re looking at and be able to match it to your Works Cited.

In-Text Citation Basics (How-To)

When you do need a formal in-text citation, here’s how MLA format works:

Author + Page Number

Include the author’s last name and page number in parentheses. No comma between them.

Jacobs describes the constant fear of discovery during her years in hiding (148).

Author Already Named

If you name the author in your sentence, just put the page number in parentheses.

According to Douglass, the fight with Covey was “the turning-point in my career as a slave” (39).

No Page Numbers

Many sources (websites, videos, public domain texts on our class site) don’t have page numbers. Just use the author’s name. If you’ve already named them in the sentence, you don’t need a parenthetical at all.

Hughes opens the poem with “I, too, sing America.”

Poems

Use line numbers instead of page numbers. Write “line” or “lines” the first time, then just the numbers after that.

Cullen asks, “What is Africa to me” (line 1).

Later, the speaker describes “the survey of all time” (10).

Videos and Films

Cite by the title (shortened if needed).

The documentary explores how the show became “a platform for political expression and the fight for social justice” (Mr. SOUL!).
