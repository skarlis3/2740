MLA Citations

In-text citations, Works Cited, and a ready-to-use list of class readings

What This Covers

This page covers the basics of MLA citation for your final project: how to do in-text citations, how to set up a Works Cited page, and a ready-to-use Works Cited list of all the course readings you can copy from directly.

In-Text Citations

Every time you quote, paraphrase, or reference a source in your writing, include a brief in-text citation so readers know where the information comes from. In MLA, in-text citations go in parentheses at the end of the sentence, before the period.

The Basics

An MLA in-text citation typically includes the author’s last name and the page number (if available). No comma between them.

Quoting:

Douglass writes, “You have seen how a man was made a slave; you shall see how a slave was made a man” (39).

Paraphrasing:

Douglass describes his fight with Covey as the turning point that restored his sense of humanity (39).

When You Name the Author in the Sentence

If you mention the author’s name in your sentence, you only need the page number in parentheses.

According to Douglass, the fight with Covey was “the turning-point in my career as a slave” (39).

No Page Numbers

Many of the sources you’ll use (websites, videos, poems, public domain texts on our class site) don’t have page numbers. In that case, just use the author’s name. If you’ve already named them in the sentence, you don’t need a parenthetical citation at all.

Hughes writes, “I, too, sing America” (Hughes).

Or, if the author is already named: Hughes opens the poem with “I, too, sing America.”

Poems

For poems, use line numbers instead of page numbers. Use the word “line” or “lines” the first time, then just the numbers after that.

Cullen asks, “What is Africa to me” (line 1).

Later in the poem, the speaker describes “the survey of all time” (10).

Videos and Films

Cite by the title (shortened if needed) since there’s usually no single author.

The documentary explores how the show became “a platform for political expression and the fight for social justice” (Mr. SOUL!).

Works Cited Page

Your Works Cited page goes at the end of your project (or as its own page on your site). It lists every source you referenced, in alphabetical order by the first word of the entry (usually the author’s last name).

Formatting

  • Title

    Center the words “Works Cited” at the top of the page. Don’t bold, underline, or enlarge it.

  • Alphabetical order

    Entries are sorted alphabetically by the first word (usually the author’s last name). Ignore “A,” “An,” and “The” when sorting titles.

  • Hanging indent

    The first line of each entry is flush left. Any additional lines are indented. (On Google Sites, you may need to approximate this with spacing — it doesn’t have to be perfect.)

  • Include working links

    For any online source, include a working URL at the end of the entry. Since this is a website project, your readers should be able to click through to verify your sources.
